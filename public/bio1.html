Classification of Animals

Grouping animals into different levels based on their common characteristics is known as classification.

Significance of classification of organisms:

  • Easy to study organisms
  • Ability to identify specific distinguishing characteristics of organisms
  • Ability to gain new insights about the whole biosphere
  • Ability to reveal different relationships among organisms
  • Identification of organisms with economic use for humans

Aristotle introduced the first scientific classification of organisms.

Carolus Linnaeus in the 18th century introduced a successful classification method.

There are two types of classification:

  • Natural classification
  • Artificial classification

In artificial classification, features such as the presence or absence of locomotive appendages or habits are considered.

It does not depend on evolutionary relationships.

Natural classification, on the other hand, depicts the evolutionary relationships among organisms.

In natural classification, morphological, physiological, and molecular biological features are considered.

Natural classification:

  • Explains the natural relationships among organisms of the same species
  • Explains the evolutionary relationships between organisms

The five-kingdom classification system was introduced by Robert Whittaker in 1969.

Modern classification, introduced by Carl Woese in 1990, includes three domains:

  • Domain Archaea
  • Domain Bacteria
  • Domain Eukarya

Organisms in Domain Archaea:

  • Prokaryotic â€“ without an organized nucleus
  • Have the ability to live in extreme environments
  • Cannot be destroyed by antibiotics

Organisms in Domain Bacteria:

  • Prokaryotic
  • Pathogenic
  • Sensitive to antibiotics
  • Can be found everywhere in the environment
  • Are the most abundant group of organisms
  • Bacteria and cyanobacteria belong to this group

Organisms in Domain Eukarya:

  • Eukaryotic cellular organisms
  • Not sensitive to antibiotics
  • Live in various environments

There are four kingdoms:

  • Kingdom Protista
  • Kingdom Fungi
  • Kingdom Animalia
  • Kingdom Plantae

Organisms belonging to Kingdom Protista possess eukaryotic cellular organization.

They are unicellular or multicellular organisms without specific tissues.

They live in environments associated with water and are mostly photosynthetic.

Some are heterotrophic.

Algae and protozoa belong to this group.

Some harmful and useful effects of Protista:

  • Algae act as the primary food source in the aquatic environment
  • Algae help in the production of lichens
  • Alginic acid extracted from algae is needed for ice cream production
  • Some protozoans cause harmful effects on humans

Kingdom Fungi is characterized by a chitinous cell wall and eukaryotic cellular organization.

Features of Bacteria:

  • Structure
  • Microscopic
  • Unicellular
  • Prokaryotic
  • Shape
  • Spherical shape
  • Rod shape
  • Spiral shape
  • Comma shape
  • Nutrition
  • Mostly heterotrophic, but cyanobacteria are autotrophic
  • Reproduction
  • Mostly perform asexual reproduction
  • Distribution
  • Almost everywhere

Features of Protista:

  • Structure
  • Most are microscopic
  • Some red algae have large bodies
  • Eukaryotic
  • Shape
  • Leaf-like
  • Horse-shaped
  • Shoe-shaped
  • Nutrition
  • Algae are autotrophic, while other organisms are heterotrophic
  • Reproduction
  • Asexual reproduction
  • Distribution
  • Marine water
  • Freshwater
  • Damp soil
  • Inside other organisms

Features of Fungi:

  • Structure
  • Microscopic
  • Mushroom reproductive organs can be seen
  • Eukaryotic
  • Shape
  • Leaf-like
  • Horse-shaped
  • Shoe-shaped
  • Nutrition
  • All are heterotrophic
  • Reproduction
  • Mostly perform asexual reproduction
  • Distribution
  • Organic matter
  • Living bodies

Plants can be divided into two categories:

  • Flowering plants
  • Non-flowering plants

Flowering plants can be further divided into:

  • Dicots
  • Monocots

Non-flowering plants can be divided into:

  • Seeded plants
  • Seedless plants

Features of non-flowering seedless plants:

  • Structure
  • Small to large-sized plants
  • Some plants lack tissue differentiation
  • No stem, leaves, and root
  • These plants are known as thallus
  • Some plants possess vascular tissue
  • Shape
  • Thalloid body or fern-like shape
  • Nutrition
  • All are autotrophic and photosynthetic
  • Reproduction
  • Asexual reproduction
  • Sexual reproduction
  • Fragmentation of vegetative parts
  • Distribution
  • Terrestrial environment with low sunlight

Features of flowering plants:

  • Structure
  • Tissue differentiation
  • Vascular tissue
  • Root, stem, and leaves are present
  • Shape
  • Large in size
  • Most of them are trees
  • Woody stems
  • Some are shrubs
  • Nutrition
  • All are autotrophic and photosynthetic
  • Reproduction
  • Sexual reproduction by seed
  • Asexual reproduction by spores
  • Distribution
  • Terrestrial environment

Features of monocots:

  • A single seed coat
  • Unbranched stems
  • Fibrous root system
  • Parallel venation
  • Trimerous flowers
  • No secondary growth
  • Shape
  • Even stem diameter

Features of dicots:

  • Two seed coats
  • Branched stems
  • Taproot system
  • Reticulate venation
  • Tetramerous flowers
  • Secondary growth
  • Broad base
  • Thin tip

Kingdom Animalia can be divided into two:

  • Vertebrates
  • Invertebrates

Vertebrates can be divided into:

  • Pisces
  • Amphibians
  • Reptiles
  • Birds
  • Mammals

Invertebrates can be divided into:

  • Phylum Porifera
  • Phylum Coelenterata
  • Phylum Platyhelminthes
  • Phylum Nematoda
  • Phylum Annelida
  • Phylum Arthropoda
  • Phylum Mollusca
  • Phylum Echinodermata

Features of Cnidaria

  • Most are marine, with a few freshwater species.
  • Multicellular body made up of two germinal layers.
  • A cavity present in the body, known as the coelenteron, acts as a digestive system.
  • There are two forms: medusa and polyp. Medusa can move, while polyps attach to surfaces.
  • They exhibit radial symmetry.
  • They use cnidocytes to paralyze small creatures and eat them.
  • Asexual reproduction is done through budding, and sexual reproduction also occurs.

Features of Annelida

  • Live in terrestrial, freshwater, and marine environments.
  • Multicellular, triploblastic, and soft-bodied animals.
  • The body is divided into a head, muscular foot, and visceral mass.
  • The body is monitored by mucus.
  • Some annelids possess internal and external shells made of calcium carbonate.
  • They exhibit sexual reproduction, and most are unisexual.

Features of Mollusca

  • Live in terrestrial, freshwater, and marine environments.
  • Multicellular, triploblastic, and soft-bodied animals.
  • The body is divided into a head, muscular foot, and visceral mass.
  • The body is monitored by mucus.
  • Some mollusks possess internal and external shells made of calcium carbonate.
  • They exhibit sexual reproduction, and most are unisexual.

Features of Arthropods

  • Live in marine, terrestrial, and freshwater environments.
  • Triploblastic, coelomate, and possess jointed limbs.
  • A chitinous cuticle on the body acts as an exoskeleton.
  • Some possess special wings.
  • The body exhibits bilateral symmetry.
  • Exhibit sexual dimorphism.
  • Reproduce sexually.

Features of Echinodermata

  • All are marine.
  • Triploblastic, coelomate organisms.
  • Possess a sharp, spiny body covering.
  • Body is star-shaped.
  • Possess a water vascular system.
  • Tube feet are used for locomotion and respiration.
  • Heart, brain, and eyes are absent.
  • Exhibit pentaradial symmetry.
  • Exhibit sexual dimorphism.
  • Reproduce sexually.

Vertebrates are organisms with a backbone.

Vertebrates can be divided into five groups:

  • Pisces
  • Amphibia
  • Reptilia
  • Aves
  • Mammalia

Features of Pisces

  • Possess a bony or cartilaginous endoskeleton.
  • Body is streamlined, which allows them to swim in water.
  • Body covered in scales.
  • Possess fins.
  • Have a two-chambered heart.
  • Respire through gills.
  • Cold-blooded animals.
  • Eyes without eyelids.

Pisces can be divided into two categories based on their exoskeleton:

  • Chondrichthyes - skeleton made of cartilage.
  • Osteichthyes - skeleton made of bone.

Features of Amphibia

  • Water is essential to complete their life cycle.
  • Possess metamorphosis.
  • Have thin, mucus-covered skin with glands.
  • Pentadactyl limbs.
  • Three-chambered heart.
  • Respire through lungs, moist skin, and the buccal cavity.
  • Cold-blooded.

Features of Reptilia

  • Dry skin without glands.
  • Possess scales on the skin.
  • Pentadactyl limbs.
  • Heart with two atria and incompletely divided ventricles.
  • Respiration through lungs.
  • Possess internal fertilization.
  • Cold-blooded animals.

Features of Aves

  • Possess a lightweight bony endoskeleton.
  • Streamlined body shape.
  • Skin covered by feathers.
  • Scales are restricted to the legs.
  • Possess a beak.
  • Pentadactyl limbs.
  • Four-chambered heart.
  • Warm-blooded.

Features of Mammalia

  • Skin covered by hair.
  • Possess mammary glands.
  • Possess ear lobes.
  • Four-chambered heart.
  • Complete double circulation.
  • Biconcave red blood cells lacking a nucleus.
  • Warm-blooded animals.
  • Testes are present outside the body.
  • Internal fertilization.
  • Possess a placenta.

Nomenclature is used to name animals scientifically.

A successful nomenclature system was introduced by a Swedish natural scientist named Carolus Linnaeus in 1753.

As it contains two epithets, it is known as binomial nomenclature.

The methodology of naming organisms is regulated by the International Code of Botanical Nomenclature (ICBN) and the International Code of Zoological Nomenclature (ICZN).

Standards of binomial nomenclature:

  • The scientific name of a species is composed of two epithets.
  • The first epithet is the genus name, and the second epithet is the specific epithet.
  • The species name is given in Latin or Greek.
  • The first letter of the genus name is capitalized.

Classification of Animals

Grouping animals into different levels based on their common characteristics is known as classification.

Significance of classification of organisms:

  • Easy to study organisms
  • Ability to identify specific distinguishing characteristics of organisms
  • Ability to gain new insights about the whole biosphere
  • Ability to reveal different relationships among organisms
  • Identification of organisms with economic use for humans

Aristotle introduced the first scientific classification of organisms.

Carolus Linnaeus in the 18th century introduced a successful classification method.

There are two types of classification:

  • Natural classification
  • Artificial classification

In artificial classification, features such as the presence or absence of locomotive appendages or habits are considered.

It does not depend on evolutionary relationships.

Natural classification, on the other hand, depicts the evolutionary relationships among organisms.

In natural classification, morphological, physiological, and molecular biological features are considered.

Natural classification:

  • Explains the natural relationships among organisms of the same species
  • Explains the evolutionary relationships between organisms

The five-kingdom classification system was introduced by Robert Whittaker in 1969.

Modern classification, introduced by Carl Woese in 1990, includes three domains:

  • Domain Archaea
  • Domain Bacteria
  • Domain Eukarya

Organisms in Domain Bacteria:

  • Prokaryotic
  • Pathogenic
  • Sensitive to antibiotics
  • Can be found everywhere in the environment
  • Are the most abundant group of organisms
  • Bacteria and cyanobacteria belong to this group

Organisms in Domain Eukarya:

  • Eukaryotic cellular organisms
  • Not sensitive to antibiotics
  • Live in various environments

There are four kingdoms:

  • Kingdom Protista
  • Kingdom Fungi
  • Kingdom Animalia
  • Kingdom Plantae

Organisms belonging to Kingdom Protista possess eukaryotic cellular organization.

They are unicellular or multicellular organisms without specific tissues.

They live in environments associated with water and are mostly photosynthetic.

Some are heterotrophic.

Algae and protozoa belong to this group.

Some harmful and useful effects of Protista:

  • Algae act as the primary food source in the aquatic environment
  • Algae help in the production of lichens
  • Alginic acid extracted from algae is needed for ice cream production
  • Some protozoans cause harmful effects on humans

Kingdom Fungi is characterized by a chitinous cell wall and eukaryotic cellular organization.

Plants can be divided into two categories:

  • Flowering plants
  • Non-flowering plants

Flowering plants can be further divided into:

  • Dicots
  • Monocots

Non-flowering plants can be divided into:

  • Seeded plants
  • Seedless plants

Features of non-flowering seedless plants:

  • Structure
  • Small to large-sized plants
  • Some plants lack tissue differentiation
  • No stem, leaves, and root
  • These plants are known as thallus
  • Some plants possess vascular tissue
  • Shape
  • Thalloid body or fern-like shape
  • Nutrition
  • All are autotrophic and photosynthetic
  • Reproduction
  • Asexual reproduction
  • Sexual reproduction
  • Fragmentation of vegetative parts
  • Distribution
  • Terrestrial environment with low sunlight

Features of flowering plants:

  • Structure

  • Shape

  • Asexual reproduction by spores
  • Distribution
  • Terrestrial environment

Nomenclature is used to name animals scientifically.

A successful nomenclature system was introduced by a Swedish natural scientist named Carolus Linnaeus in 1753.

As it contains two epithets, it is known as binomial nomenclature.

The methodology of naming organisms is regulated by the International Code of Botanical Nomenclature (ICBN) and the International Code of Zoological Nomenclature (ICZN).

Standards of binomial nomenclature:

  • The scientific name of a species is composed of two epithets.
  • The first epithet is the genus name, and the second epithet is the specific epithet.
  • The species name is given in Latin or Greek.
  • The first letter of the genus name is capitalized.

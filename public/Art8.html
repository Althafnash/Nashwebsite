Social Proof

A simple experiment carried out in the 1950s by legendary psychologist Solomon Asch shows how peer pressure can warp common sense.

A subject is shown a line drawn on paper and next to it three lines—numbered 1, 2, and 3—one shorter, one longer, and one the same size as the original.

He or she must indicate which of the three lines corresponds to the original one.

If the person is alone in the room, he or she gives the correct answer because the task is really simple.

Now, other five people enter the room, whom the subject does not know.

One after the other, they give the wrong answer, saying number 1 or number 2, although it is very clear that number 3 is the correct answer.

Then, in the subject’s turn again, in one-third of the time, he or she will answer incorrectly to match the other persons’ responses.

Why do we act like this? Well, in the past, following others was a good survival strategy.

Imagine that fifty thousand years ago you were traveling around the Serengeti with your hunter-gatherer friends, and suddenly everyone starts to bolt.

What would you have done? Would you have stayed put, scratching your head and weighing up whether what you were looking at was a lion or something that looked like a lion but was, in fact, a harmless animal?

No, you would have sprinted after your friends. Later on, when you were safe, you could have reflected on what had actually happened.

Those who acted differently—and I’m sure there were some—would have exited the gene pool.

We are direct heirs of those who copied others’ behavior.

This pattern is so deeply rooted in us that we still use it today, even when it offers no survival advantage.
